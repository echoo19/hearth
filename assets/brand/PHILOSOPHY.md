# Kept Flame

*The design philosophy behind the Hearth mark.*

A hearth is not a fire. It is the stone that holds the fire — the built thing,
the patient architecture that makes warmth safe to live beside. This
philosophy begins there: the vessel is drawn, and the flame is what remains.
Presence rendered as absence. The mark is one solid, quiet form with a living
counter-shape cut from its center, the way a keyhole holds a key or a door
holds the light of the room behind it. Nothing is added to say *fire*; space
is simply kept for it.

Form is architectural before it is organic. The outer silhouette carries the
discipline of masonry — a flat, grounded base, walls that rise plumb, a
single arch closing overhead with the even certainty of a compass stroke.
Against this stillness, the counter-form is the only organic gesture
permitted: one asymmetric flame, leaning as real fire leans, its left edge
hollowed by draft, its tip pulled toward the arch. The tension between the
two — drawn stone, breathing absence — is the entire composition. One
geometry, one gesture, nothing else.

Color behaves like heat, not decoration. The mark lives as a single ink:
ember on dark, soot on light, and it must survive both without alteration.
When warmth is wanted, it comes as a slow vertical gradation inside the
silhouette — the deep red of banked coals rising into struck-match orange —
never as effects, shadows, or glow. Restraint here is what lets the small
sizes burn clean: at sixteen pixels the mark must still read as a doorway
with a fire inside, meticulously legible, nothing lost.

Every curve is calibrated, not sketched. Radii agree with one another;
junctions land on the grid; the flame's cusps are tuned by eye and then tuned
again, the way a typeface's counters are tuned — this is the product of
countless passes by a steady hand, and it should look like it. Whitespace is
structural: the clearspace around the mark is part of the mark, a hearthstone
apron that no other element may cross. Scale changes nothing; the form is one
idea, and one idea survives any size.

The mark keeps a quiet double reading for those who live in terminals: the
arch is also a doorway left open — an engine that lets others in — and the
kept flame is whatever they bring to it. It never announces this. Like all
good marks it simply sits, warm and exact, looking as if it took a very long
time to make. Because it did.
